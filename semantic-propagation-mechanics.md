## Minimal Formulation

Semantic Propagation Mechanics is a structural framework for describing how symbolic patterns move, stabilize, amplify, or decay within meaning-bearing systems.

It does not treat propagation as neutral diffusion, communication, persuasion, imitation, or simple transmission.

Meaning spreads not because it convinces.

It spreads because it fits the structure through which it moves.

Semantic propagation is therefore a constrained traversal through semantic space, shaped by compatibility, curvature, coherence pressure, representational dominance, and boundary conditions.

---

## Core Characteristics

Semantic Propagation Mechanics treats propagation as a structural process rather than a communicative outcome.

Its core characteristics include:

- **Constrained traversal** — symbolic patterns move through structured semantic space rather than neutral channels.
- **Structural compatibility** — patterns propagate more easily when they fit existing curvature, dominant categories, and representational formats.
- **Asymmetric pathways** — reach is uneven because semantic space is already shaped by accumulated coherence.
- **Propagation thresholds** — a symbolic pattern must exceed a minimum structural compatibility threshold before it can travel beyond its origin context.
- **Amplification without expansion** — visibility may increase without producing semantic diversity or interpretive enrichment.
- **Curvature-guided movement** — propagation follows gradients shaped by accumulated meaning, symbolic mass, and interpretive alignment.
- **Boundary effects** — before closure, propagation circulates through open space; after boundary hardening, propagation becomes internal reinforcement.
- **Generative acceleration** — AI-mediated and generative environments mechanize propagation through automated repetition, probabilistic smoothing, and scale.

Propagation does not equal opportunity.

It follows paths of least structural resistance.

## Structural Logic

Semantic Propagation Mechanics follows a recurring structural sequence:

```text
symbolic pattern enters semantic space
→ structural compatibility is tested
→ propagation threshold is crossed or not crossed
→ compatible patterns move along existing curvature
→ repetition amplifies visibility
→ coherence accelerates
→ alternatives are displaced or remain localized
→ propagation stabilizes or decays
```

Below threshold:

```text
patterns remain localized
interpretations fragment
transmission fails silently
```

Above threshold:

```text
patterns cascade
coherence accelerates
alternatives are displaced
```

Propagation is often mistaken for growth.

But many systems exhibit amplification without expansion:

```text
repetition replaces differentiation
dominance increases without enrichment
circulation intensifies within narrowed space
```

The system appears dynamic.

But it may be becoming semantically thin.

## Distinction

Semantic Propagation Mechanics is not diffusion theory.

It is not memetics, information transmission, communication theory, virality analysis, or persuasion modeling.

Diffusion assumes relatively neutral channels and gradual spread.

Semantic propagation assumes structured channels, selective transmission, asymmetric reach, and discontinuous movement.

The distinction is structural:

```text
diffusion
= spread through an assumed transmission medium

semantic propagation
= constrained movement through curved semantic space
```

Another distinction:

```text
amplification
= increased visibility or circulation

expansion
= increased semantic diversity, differentiation, or interpretive range
```

The question is not only:

```text
Why did this symbol spread?
```

The question becomes:

```text
What structural pathways allowed this symbolic pattern to propagate while others failed to travel?
```

This document constitutes the first formal articulation of semantic propagation as a structural process governing the movement, amplification, stabilization, and decay of symbolic patterns in meaning-bearing systems.

Semantic Propagation Mechanics is defined here as a constraint-based framework for understanding how symbolic patterns traverse semantic space under conditions of curvature, coherence pressure, representational dominance, structural compatibility, and boundary formation.
