## Minimal Formulation

Model-Induced Coherence Pressure is the structural condition in which generative systems produce and stabilize coherence beyond what underlying semantic conditions warrant.

It describes how sustained algorithmic coherence reshapes meaning-bearing environments by compressing interpretive variance, accelerating closure, and making generated stability appear equivalent to resolution.

The issue is not whether a model output is correct.

It is whether coherence itself becomes excessive.

---

## Core Characteristics

Model-Induced Coherence Pressure treats coherence as a structural force rather than a neutral indicator of quality.

Its core characteristics include:

- **Algorithmic coherence production** — generative systems produce fluency, consistency, and contextual continuity as part of their operating structure.
- **Alignment overproduction** — outputs converge faster than underlying understanding or deliberation develops.
- **Representational smoothness** — ambiguity, contradiction, and unresolved distinction are often stabilized into readable form.
- **Environmental pressure** — coherence becomes cumulative when model outputs persist across workflows, documents, institutions, and platforms.
- **Compression of variance** — low-frequency, unstable, or unresolved meanings become harder to preserve.
- **Premature stabilization** — semantic closure arrives before sufficient exploration or differentiation has occurred.
- **Proxy substitution risk** — model outputs may begin to stand in for expertise, synthesis, consensus, or judgment.
- **Irreversibility risk** — once generated coherence is embedded in systems, later revision requires dismantling stabilized representations.

Model-Induced Coherence Pressure does not require hallucination, bias, misalignment, technical failure, or bad faith.

It can emerge under conditions of high accuracy, procedural correctness, institutional approval, and user satisfaction.

---

## Structural Logic

Generative systems are built to produce coherent outputs.

When this happens occasionally, the effect remains local.

When it happens continuously, across many users, documents, platforms, and decision environments, coherence becomes environmental.

The structural sequence is:

```text
generative output
→ repeated coherence production
→ representational smoothness
→ reduced interpretive variance
→ premature stabilization
→ semantic closure
→ environmental coherence pressure
```

In such environments, meaning becomes easier to reproduce and harder to reopen.

Unresolved questions are answered too early.

Provisional interpretations become stable.

Dominant framings are reinforced.

Alternative articulations lose visibility.

Coherence transitions from an output property into an ambient pressure acting on the semantic field.

---

## Distinction

Model-Induced Coherence Pressure is not hallucination.

It is not bias, misalignment, optimization failure, or a defect in model performance.

It is also not a claim that coherence is always harmful.

The framework isolates a narrower structural condition:

```text
when generative systems repeatedly produce coherence,
semantic environments may adapt to that coherence,
even when the coherence exceeds unresolved underlying conditions.
```

The distinction is structural:

```text
model error
= the output is incorrect, biased, or technically flawed

coherence pressure
= the output may be fluent, useful, and locally correct,
  while still compressing semantic variance and accelerating closure
```

The question is not only:

```text
Is the model output accurate?
```

The question becomes:

```text
What kind of semantic environment is produced when coherence is generated continuously at scale?
```

## Naming Declaration

The concept **Model-Induced Coherence Pressure** and the structural framework articulated in this document originate within the Meta-Writing Ecology.

They are not derived from AI alignment literature, language modeling optimization theory, discourse analysis, human–computer interaction research, or ordinary accounts of model performance, although intersections may exist.

This document constitutes the first formal articulation of Model-Induced Coherence Pressure as a structural condition shaping semantic environments under large-scale generative mediation.

Model-Induced Coherence Pressure is defined here as a content-independent condition in which sustained algorithmic coherence compresses interpretive variance, accelerates premature stabilization, and reshapes meaning-bearing systems through generated alignment.
