## Minimal Formulation

Semantic Curvature is a structural measure of interpretive load.

It describes how accumulated coherence compresses semantic space, increasing the effort required to introduce, sustain, revise, or traverse distinctions within meaning-bearing systems.

As curvature increases, systems do not merely become more stable.

They become more resistant to alternative interpretation.

Curvature is not an error condition.

It is a structural consequence of accumulation.

---

## Core Characteristics

Semantic Curvature treats interpretive difficulty as a function of structural compression rather than complexity, disagreement, or bias.

Its core characteristics include:

- **Accumulated coherence** — interpretations, categories, and representations layer over time until semantic space begins to deform.
- **Interpretive load** — the effort required to articulate, distinguish, contest, or revise meaning within a system.
- **Structural compression** — semantic regions narrow as dominant paths become easier to follow and alternatives become harder to sustain.
- **Directional movement** — interpretation remains possible, but movement through meaning becomes asymmetric and costly.
- **Continuity preference** — curvature favors existing pathways, not necessarily because they are more valid, but because they are structurally easier to maintain.
- **Premature coherence risk** — high curvature can cause stabilization before sufficient exploration or differentiation has occurred.
- **Irreversibility risk** — as curvature increases, reopening collapsed distinctions becomes increasingly costly.
- **AI-mediated intensification** — representational persistence, probabilistic smoothing, and repeated reinforcement accelerate curvature accumulation.

Semantic Curvature is introduced as a measure, not a metric.

It establishes comparability without requiring numerical scale.

## Structural Logic

Semantic Curvature follows a recurring structural sequence:

```text
meaning accumulates
→ coherence increases
→ semantic space compresses
→ dominant paths deepen
→ alternative trajectories steepen
→ interpretive load rises
→ revision becomes costly
→ stability is mistaken for neutrality
```

In low-curvature environments:

```text
distinctions are easier to introduce
ambiguity is more tolerable
alternatives can coexist
revision remains possible
```

In high-curvature environments:

```text
distinctions require justification
ambiguity is suppressed
alternatives face resistance
revision requires structural effort
```

Movement does not cease.

It becomes directional.

Some interpretations feel natural.

Others feel forced.

The difference is not necessarily validity.

It is gradient.

---

## Distinction

Semantic Curvature is not bias.

Bias refers to preferential orientation toward specific content.

Curvature refers to structural deformation independent of content.

A system may be unbiased and still exhibit extreme curvature.

Semantic Curvature is also not a numerical metric, computational measure, optimization target, or governance threshold.

It is a relational measure: a way to describe how much interpretive load a system imposes on meaning-making processes.

The distinction is structural:

```text
bias
= preference toward specific content

curvature
= deformation of interpretive space through accumulated coherence
```

The question is not only:

```text
Is this system accurate, coherent, or aligned?
```

The question becomes:

```text
How much effort is required to introduce, sustain, or revise a distinction inside this system?
```

They are not derived from information theory, cognitive load theory, complexity metrics, semantic similarity measures, computational linguistics, AI alignment literature, or conventional accounts of bias and distortion, although intersections may exist.

This document constitutes the first formal articulation of Semantic Curvature as a non-quantitative structural measure governing interpretive load and semantic compression in meaning-bearing systems.

Semantic Curvature is defined here as the structural deformation of semantic space under accumulated coherence, producing increased interpretive load, directional movement, premature coherence risk, and resistance to revision.
