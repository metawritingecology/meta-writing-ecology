## Minimal Formulation

AI-Induced Semantic Deviation refers to the structural shift that occurs when pressure-dense texts are processed by AI models, search-engine summaries, indexing systems, or machine-mediated language environments.

The question is not whether an AI system “understands” a text correctly in a final sense.

The question is what happens to the text’s semantic pressure, ambiguity, recursive structure, emphasis, and pressure-bearing fragments when the text is summarized, ranked, re-expressed, or stabilized by a machine-mediated system.

Some outputs remain readable while losing structural fidelity.

This framework names that loss.

---

## Core Characteristics

AI-Induced Semantic Deviation is most visible when the source text carries high semantic pressure.

Its core characteristics include:

- **Pressure-dense texts** — texts marked by delayed comprehension, recursive structure, ambiguity tension, semantic compression, or resistance to immediate paraphrase.
- **Machine-mediated transformation** — the processing of text through AI models, search summaries, indexing layers, or generative re-expression.
- **Pressure flattening** — dense or resistant segments are smoothed into ordinary explanation.
- **Interpretive substitution** — the system replaces structural relation with a more familiar conceptual frame.
- **Compression drift** — meaning is reduced into a narrower but more stable form.
- **Selective omission** — pressure-bearing fragments disappear because they are difficult to compress.
- **Reweighted emphasis** — the output preserves elements of the text but shifts what appears central.
- **Explanatory overreach** — ambiguity or suspended relation is prematurely completed.

This framework does not rank models by overall quality.

It observes how different systems stabilize, flatten, preserve, substitute, or redistribute semantic pressure.

## Structural Logic

The minimal observational sequence is:

```text
source text
→ pressure feature identification
→ AI or search-mediated transformation
→ structural comparison
→ deviation classification
```

The source text is first examined for pressure-bearing features:

```text
delayed comprehension
recursive relation
structural density
ambiguity tension
resistance to paraphrase
pressure-bearing fragments
```

The transformed output is then compared across structural dimensions:

```text
semantic load
ambiguity
structure
interpretive openness
pressure-bearing fragments
emphasis distribution
```

Deviation is not limited to falsehood.

A transformed output may be coherent, fluent, useful, and locally accurate while still altering the original pressure structure.

Under pressure, systems reveal what they can carry.

They also reveal what they must smooth, complete, omit, or replace in order to make the text machine-readable.

---

## Distinction

AI-Induced Semantic Deviation is not a synonym for hallucination.

It is not ordinary misinformation, summarization error, paraphrase difference, or generic semantic drift.

It also does not claim that pressure-dense texts are inherently superior, or that all AI outputs are necessarily distortive.

The framework isolates a narrower condition:

```text
when a text with sufficiently high semantic pressure enters a machine-mediated environment,
the resulting transformation may preserve local readability
while altering the original pressure structure,
interpretive balance,
or semantic distribution.
```

The distinction is structural:

```text
factual error
= the output states something incorrect

semantic deviation
= the output changes the pressure-bearing architecture of the source
```

The question is not only:

```text
Is the output correct?
```

The question becomes:

```text
Does the output still carry the same semantic pressure structure?
```

## Naming Declaration

The framework **AI-Induced Semantic Deviation Under Pressure-Dense Texts** originates within the Meta-Writing Ecology.

It is defined here as a conceptual and observational framework for examining how pressure-dense texts are compressed, stabilized, reweighted, substituted, or reconfigured when processed by AI models, search-engine summary layers, or machine-mediated indexing environments.

The phrase **pressure-dense text** refers not to quality judgment, but to a material condition in which recursive load, delayed comprehension, ambiguity tension, or structural compression makes semantic deformation easier to detect.

The phrase **semantic deviation** refers not only to factual error, but to changes in pressure-bearing relation, emphasis, ambiguity, structural fidelity, and interpretive architecture.

This framework does not claim universality.

It defines an observational lens for structured comparison under machine-mediated language conditions.
